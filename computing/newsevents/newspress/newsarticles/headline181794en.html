In the Spotlight: Lucas Rizzo

‌

‌

Name: Lucas Rizzo

Research Topics: Explainable AI, automated reasoning, knowledge-based systems, data science

About: Lucas Rizzo is originally from Belo Horizonte - Brazil. He is an assistant lecturer in the School of Computer Science and recent Ph.D. graduate in Artificial Intelligence from TU Dublin. The title of his research project was ‘Evaluating the impact of defeasible argumentation as a modelling technique for reasoning under uncertainty'. He has a BSc in Computational Mathematics and a master’s degree in Computer Science from Universidade Federal de Minas Gerais (UFMG - Brazil). Before joining the School of Computer Science, he has worked in the fields of constraint programming and operational research, with four years of industry experience. Currently, his research focus is on automated reasoning, knowledge-based systems and explainable AI.

What inspired you to get into the field Computer Science?
My first qualification was in mathematics applied to computer science; hence I was originally interested in putting into use the concepts and ideas I had learned while studying math in school. In computer science I have focused on operational research and artificial intelligence, two subfields that apply heavy mathematical reasoning in order to solve real-world problems.

Can you tell us about your current research work?
My current research work is focused on knowledge-based systems and explainable AI, but also bringing that into a machine learning perspective that is so relevant nowadays. More specifically, I am researching predictive models based on domain knowledge which can be interpreted and understood by experts or lay people. There is a knowledge gap when evaluating the capacity of such models to exploit numerical data to perform predictions, and how they compare against each other or against other data-driven models.

What drives you to do research in this area?
My focus on explainable AI is through knowledge-based systems. These systems rely mainly on domain knowledge gathered from human experts. My interest in this area comes from understanding how people reason and solve problems, and how we can automate that through the construction of computational models. It is a very challenging problem, which demands techniques from different areas, such as knowledge engineer, logic, human computer interaction, data structures, and algorithm design.

You recently joined the teaching staff here at TU Dublin, how have you found the recent move to teaching online?
Recently I have taught programming and operating systems. Given the nature of these subjects, I think that teaching them online doesn’t have a big impact on the content being delivered. The biggest challenge is to keep students engaged while not being able to evaluate their responses in real-time. I believe this requires an extra effort from both the lecturer and the students. In my case I have tried to request more feedback through exercises and using more extensively the tools provided in the university’s VLE.

You are part of the organising committee for the upcoming AICS conference held this December, can you tell us what to expect at this year’s conference?
I had the chance to participate before in AICS 2018, and it was a great opportunity to present in an Irish venue and find out what colleagues were working on. This year’s conference is the first one to be held online. We are trying to run it as most similar as possible to previous years, maintaining a 2-day schedule of presentations and similar deadlines for submissions. Unfortunately, it won’t be possible to meet participants face-to-face, but we also expect it will allow more researchers to participate, since they can join from anyplace. Our first keynote speaker has been confirmed, Francesca Toni, a professor from Imperial College London, and one of the most recognized researchers in the area of computational argumentation.

What’s next on the horizon in terms of your research?
Explainable AI is gaining a lot of attention due to new restrictions on how companies can use people‘s data, for instance with the GDPR legislation, but also in sensible fields, such as medicine and law, in which humans are responsible for the decisions made, regardless if they had some algorithmic aid. I believe the future for the field and for my research is quite challenging but also very rewarding. Automated reasoning and knowledge-based systems are one way of tackling the explainability problem in AI, but regardless of the approach the goal is the same, to make AI more transparent and trustworthy for their end-users.

TU Dublin Computer Science. September 2020.
